A Different Kind of Work

Imagine if your job
was piloting a robot.

Not science fiction. Not someday. The infrastructure for this already exists. What's missing is the deployment.

The current deal

Show up. Push your body through it. Repeat until it breaks.

Back pain. Knee replacements. Repetitive stress injuries.

Decades of physical wear traded for just enough money to keep going. And no real alternative.

The emerging alternative

Pilot a robot from home. The robot does the work. You keep the paycheck.

Teleoperation: you guide a robot through tasks in real time. The skill and judgment are still yours.

The body that pays the price is not. Already deployed in factories. Next: everywhere else.

What if meaningful work
didn't cost you your body?

Most physically damaging jobs are ones robots can do now.

The missing piece isn't the technology. It's a human operator who gives a damn.

Jobs that could be done remotely, today or soon

Meal prep for homebound people
  • Elderly or disabled people who can't cook for themselves
  • You pilot a robot arm in their kitchen from anywhere
  • Hot meals. Real food. Human care. No commute.
Deployable now
Yard work and property maintenance
  • Mowing, pruning, debris clearing, gutter cleaning
  • No crew needs to show up physically
  • A wheeled robot + a good operator handles most of it from home
Deployable now
Road maintenance and infrastructure
  • Pothole patching, line painting, sign replacement, debris removal
  • Dangerous and repetitive — exactly what robots are for
  • Operator stays safe. Work still gets done right.
Near-term
Construction and skilled trades
  • Framing, concrete, electrical rough-in, roofing
  • The most body-punishing work on the planet
  • The knowledge stays yours. The physical cost doesn't have to.
On the horizon

Mobile ALOHA doing real household work. Cooking, cleaning, loading the dishwasher. This is what teleoperation leads to. Stanford University

This is not about replacing workers. It's about giving workers a body that doesn't break.

The job is the same. The cost to the person doing it is not.

The Window

It's not whether
AI compounds.
It's how fast.

The relevant question for planning purposes is not whether AI capability continues to compound. The evidence strongly suggests it will.

The disagreement among researchers is about speed, not direction. The window to prepare is before these shifts become mainstream, not after they already have.

See the evidence

Soft Takeoff

Years of gradual compounding

Institutions adapt. Regulatory frameworks catch up. Most people have time to watch and adjust. Planning cycles can handle the pace of change.

Hard Takeoff

Months, maybe weeks

Once the recursive improvement loop closes, compounding happens faster than institutions can respond. Most people notice after the world has already changed.

01 Free

Start making content (responsibly)

Film what you learn, build, and figure out. The One-Hour Rule: if it took you more than an hour to learn, it could be a video. Most people skip this because it feels too simple.

Don't share location, family, financial, or safety-sensitive details publicly.

02 5K - 30K

Find land or space

Land prices vary enormously by region and parcel. Owner-builder, reclaimed, and adaptive-reuse pathways exist in many places. The rural cost curve has not matched the urban explosion.

Each carries different legal and code regimes. Work with licensed local professionals before any purchase.

03 25K - 100K

Build a Shop

Not a house. A shouse: shop first, shelter second. Steel kit prices swing with commodity markets. The structure becomes the base for robotics, production, storage, and eventually living space.

Habitability conversions face local codes that vary by county. Consult a licensed contractor first.

04 2K - 15K

Build, Film, Automate, Teach

Use the space. Document what you build. Open-source projects worth knowing: AhaRobot ($1K dual-arm home robot), Mobile ALOHA (proven bimanual manipulation), Hugging Face LeRobot (AI for robot arms), FarmBot (CNC food-growing), and open-weight LLMs.

Do your own research. Laws and safety requirements vary.
